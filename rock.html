Ivan Fyodorovich Karamazov (Vanka)

Иван Карамазов

Limestone

“But Ivan loves nobody. Ivan is not one of us. People like Ivan are not our sort, my boy. They are like a cloud of dust. When the wind blows the dust will be gone.” (165)
“I’m afraid of Ivan. I’m more afraid of Ivan than the other.” (136)

Cold, solid, stoic: limestone and Ivan share an austere nature that is a stark contrast to the sensualist Karamazovs who live in slavery to their passions. Ivan appears distant and unsympathetic like the jagged rocks that form the foundations of reason so that the emotional carnal people can run whimsically on top and live their hedonistic lives. Ivan’s father cautions that “Ivan is not one of us” as he lacks “love.” In the end, the rational will “blow” away and be “gone” and those who truly felt all of life will thrive (165). Over many years, erosion wears the rocks away and who is there to notice? Ivan contains a profound intellect and abilities, but he has no passion and thus makes no lasting impression with others. It is these standoffish qualities that cause Fyodor to remark that he is “more afraid of Ivan than the other”, the other being Mitya who had just struck his father in the face and drew blood (136). Mitya was violent, but he was a man in all senses known to his father. They were cut from the same tumultuous cloth and the emotions and violence established understanding and humanity. Ivan had no visible emotions, no humanity, and this cold-heartedness invoked fear because Ivan could rationalize anything.
